The concept of decoloniality has existed for as long as coloniality (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). The two are so intrinsically intertwined - and despite being suppressed - decoloniality finds itself constantly creeping through the gaps of history and bursting into current affairs. In terms of knowledge, decoloniality finds itself a necessary opposition to the monetized industry of tertiary education across the globe, particularly in South Africa. The concept of decoloniality, tackled through knowledge, begins with the questioning of the validity of existing Eurocentric and Westernized knowledge (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018) and challenging the detachment of the known from the knower (Mbembe, 2015). By opening the geopolitical spheres - and accepting the pluriversal (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018) - that have long been cast aside because of the glorification of western beliefs, will allow us to redefine the bounds of knowledge across the globe and cultures, and in doing so we create a space where coloniality’s effects on the future become less gripping (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). increasing number of unresolved issues caused by coloniality (Mbembe, 2015) – the power of the self comes into play, and each person becomes responsible for their own increasing number of unresolved issues caused by coloniality (Mbembe, 2015) – the power of the self comes into play, and each person becomes responsible for their ownincreasing number of unresolved issues caused by coloniality (Mbembe, 2015) – the power of the self However, the colonial matrix of power (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018), in turn, finds ways to redefine such existing knowledge – when presented with its overwhelming popularity and validity - in a way that promotes the view of the subjugating power and diminishes the cultures/minorities from which it stems. The ability of the subjugating power to “cherry-pick” the minority populations, damages the manner of addressing the existing validity of Western knowledge. It leaves those affected by coloniality facing an endless influx of antagonism without allowing for the past moments of such to be resolved (Mbembe, 2015). It in turn leaves the individual to address and challenge the colonial power matrix, and in doing so, creates radicality. In the South African context, and within the tertiary sphere, we saw the fruition of such in the 2015 Rhodes Must Fall Movement (Mbembe, 2015). Particularly the differences between the opinions of Lorde and Gordon & Gordon (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). Where Lorde believes that true change cannot occur without dismantling the existing matrix of power – which is now upheld by the binaries of race, sex and nature (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018) – Gordon & Gordon believe that instead of completely starting over, we should use the existing tools and create from them a large enough, undisputable hegemony that overturns the matrix of power (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). Although contradictory, both these views exist in response to the advancing effects of coloniality (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). Despite both Lorde and Gordon & Gordon pose valid points, I find that their opinions very detached from the current African, and South African context. Within our context, we find ourselves between radical Africanization (Mbembe, 2015), self-righteous agreement with the perspective – and no following action - of Gordon & Gordon (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018), all while under the rule of the postcolonial middle class who have corruptly assimilated colonial thought (Mbembe, 2015). Given the working of the colonial matrix of power, and with the consideration of the unresolved antagonism of Apartheid and the implications of Africanization unto the foreigner (Mbembe, 2015) – I find it hard to conclude a collective way forward through the repercussions of coloniality on this country. In the case of the tertiary sphere, the movement of the view of students to consumers, and the shift in focus from knowledge acquirement to credit acquirement (Mbembe, 2015), the focus on Western imitation within the African context will only cause more antagonistic results. By moving tertiary education – which has now become necessary in permeating the working world created by the capitalistic society (a result of colonial reach) – to a marketable product (Mbembe, 2015), tertiary institutions in South Africa – and globally - hinder the progress of decoloniality. In efforts to correct or steer away from this in the South African context – with the mindset of Gordon & Gordon – we can, as Mbembe states, “Yes, if we fully embrace our own location in the African continent and stop thinking in South-Africa-centric terms”. By redefining higher learning and integrating the pluriverses of Africa (Mbembe, 2015) into the tertiary sphere as a means of resignification – we can nurture a holistic African house of thought, with a mastery that transcends the Anglo-American means of tertiary approach. By unsettling the Western idea of a single temporality (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018), we can engage in a field of knowledge that allows for the decolonization of the self, by the self – with a means of channeling the radicality that stems from the oppressive antagonism of the results of coloniality – not on in South Africa, but on the African continent. And although I do not know, yet, how to move forward as a whole country – addressing the higher education system in the manner proposed by Mbembe – not only will allow provide the basis for such a solution to - but it will also shift the balance of power away from the Western and Eurocentric ideals – while also allowing for the knower and known to become more closely related. The focus on decolonizing, and achieving accessible knowledge will allow for the pluriverse to exist, and open to consideration, the positions of different geo-politicalstances and opinions. And there, we start to see how the colonial matric of power, begins to fall apart.
