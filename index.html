The concept of decoloniality has existed for as long as coloniality (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). The two are so intrinsically intertwined - and despite being suppressed - decoloniality finds itself constantly creeping through the gaps of history and bursting into current affairs. In terms of knowledge, decoloniality finds itself a necessary opposition to the monetized industry of tertiary education across the globe, particularly in South Africa. ‘Blackness for Sale’ was a 10-day live eBay auction event by composer, and artist, Keith Townsend Obadike. In this piece, Obadike, objectified and placed what is his ‘blackness’ for sale on - what was relatively still new at the time – an eCommerce site. The online act was removed four days after receiving twelve bids while live, being deemed inappropriate by the hosting site (Driscoll, 2020). What was initially a comment on the forceful nature of the rising World Wide Web (Croal, 2001), became intrinsically commentative on the interaction between the virtual space and black bodies, as well as the intersectionality of sex and gender, and the complexity of blackness’ history (Driscoll, 2020), and it’s ongoing relationship with coloniality. In Miao Ying’s piece, “Blind Spot”, Ying highlights concerns, and comments on the highly censored state of accessible internet in her home country China. What started as scholastic research, lead to the eventual creation of Ying’s physical, web affected artdictionary. Ying took on the intense task of individually searching each word in the dictionary and erasing along the way the words were censored and restricted by the country’s local laws. During the making of this piece, Ying aimed to place a spotlight on this rigid policing and the negative effects of the state’s laws that are perpetuated by communist ideals. The prevalence of technology with the shift into the COVID pandemic has become paramount to the continuity of the working world, as well as in the means of youth education across the globe. However, the pre-existing, and ever expanding, digital divide presented by the vast difference in objects of affordances across various countries, has had a tumultuous effect on the already marginalized groups of various backgrounds, creating a need to address the lack of access in rural communities and disadvantaged minorities. The concept of decoloniality, tackled through knowledge, begins with the questioning of the validity of existing Eurocentric and Westernized knowledge (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018) and challenging the detachment of the known from the knower (Mbembe, 2015). By opening the geopolitical spheres - and accepting the pluriversal (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018) - that have long been cast aside because of the glorification of western beliefs, will allow us to redefine the bounds of knowledge across the globe and cultures, and in doing so we create a space where coloniality’s effects on the future become less gripping (Walsh & Mignolo, 2018). Obadike’s online performance received various responses – some directly in question of the piece, particularly in terms of the relation of value of his blackness and it’s transferability across genders (Croal, 2001). Participants particularly wanted to know what other “perks” were covered by the transfer of his blackness, and if there would be considerations to the cost in terms of its intersectionality with sexuality and gender (Croal, 2001). In response, Obadike noted that the commodification of blackness is heavily influenced by gender – and so too, it would have complicated his piece to add in more context regarding these intersections (Obadike, 2001). Obadike also noted that the performance’s lack of visual aids, allowed for the piece was able to be fetishized and resonated with audiences differently from his other works (Obadike, 2001) – and that with the inclusion and regard of gender and sex, the piece would probably not have permeated in the same way (Obadike, 2001). The existence of ‘Blind Spot’ can be considered an artifact as it crosses the two temporal spaces in its finality (Taylor, 2009). The dictionary itself, being a version pre-communist China, and its now censored state showcasing the new acceptable discourses and language. Ying’s elaborate address of this exploitation of the ideal of upholding the whole of society, also showcases the temporal shift from pre- to post-communist China (Net Art Anthology, 2007). However, this shift was not just that of the state, but also of the population’s acceptance of these censorship regulation, and what Ying refers to as ‘self-censorship’ (ELEPHANT, 2021) – something Ying relates to Stockholm Syndrome.
