Analysis of ‘Blackness for Sale’

‘Blackness for Sale’ was a 10-day live eBay auction event by composer, and artist, Keithh Townsend Obadike. In this piece, Obadike, objectified and placed his ‘blackness’ for sale on - what was relatively still new at the time – an eCommerce site. The online act was removed four days after receiving twelve bids while live, being deemed inappropriate by the hosting site (Driscoll, 2020). What was initially a comment on the forceful nature of the rising World Wide Web (Croal, 2001), became intrinsically commentative on the interaction between the virtual space and black bodies, as well as the intersectionality of sex and gender, and the complexity of blackness’ history (Driscoll, 2020), and it’s ongoing relationship with coloniality.

Obadike’s online performance received various responses – some directly in question of the piece, particularly in terms of the relation of value of his blackness and it’s transferability across genders (Croal, 2001). Participants particularly wanted to know what other “perks” were covered by the transfer of his blackness, and if there would be considerations to the cost in terms of its intersectionality with sexuality and gender (Croal, 2001). In response, Obadike noted that the commodification of blackness is heavily influenced by gender – and so too, it would have complicated his piece to add in more context regarding these intersections (Obadike, 2001). Obadike also noted that the performance’s lack of visual aids, allowed for the piece was able to be fetishized and resonated with audiences differently from his other works (Obadike, 2001) – and that with the inclusion and regard of gender and sex, the piece would probably not have permeated in the same way (Obadike, 2001).

Another aspect that Obadike commented on, was the colonial impact of the World Wide Web and how the Euro-colonialist narrative (Obadike, 2001) dominated the Web and defined the position of black bodies in doing so. The blatant naming of sites – such as Explorer, Navigator (at the time), and even now Safari – and the way they “take you to explore” (Obadike, 2001) other sites like Amazon and eBay, was already showcasing how the move into the virtual space was already moving forward to re-commodify the black body (Driscoll, 2020). When questioned about how this could be impacted by the historical and current happens of black people profiting from selling their own cultures, Obadike states that it is, “nothing new” (Obadike, 2001), but also acknowledging, that with the progression of history and current events, that there is definite complications presented because blackness is no longer just being marketed to the white gaze, but to black people and other people of color across the globe (Obadike, 2001).

Blackness is no longer being defined by its lack of presence in the difference spheres of technology and digital society (Brock, 2020). But it’s presence as a commodity has become more prevalence than ever, especially with the rise of social media app, TikTok. In recent years of fame, the app has become infamous for its suppressive community-based algorithm, that uplifts the commodified use of blackness used by the white community for financial and fame gain, while simultaneously not giving any credit to the original artists (Pruitt-Young, 2021) – of which a majority are black (McClay, 2021). In doing this, the app also garnered a lot of scrutiny for its extensive removal of black creator content, particularly in the cases where the content pertained to racial injustice (Chen, 2021) – however, it had done nothing for the extensive bulling and harassment present in the responses to the videos, or messages received by the black creators (Chen, 2021), or the strings of racially inappropriate content of extremist and racists on the white spectrum of the app (Chen, 2021).

In contrast to Obadike’s performative piece, the now inseparable virtual world has permeated black culture (Brock, 2020), and the way in which blackness is marketed, to an extent where we no longer are curious about what blackness has to offer, but rather, who using what it has to offer. Day-to-day we are seeing the comparatives between different blackness’ and how its commodification is different when affected by the intersections of sex, sexuality, and gender. The commodification of blackness presented by Obadike’s piece (Rhizome Archive, n.d.), has become an extensive basis for the profits of Web culture – not only by the social media community – but in the way it still resembles the colonial past of blackness. Obadike also noted in his interview with Fusco that, the Web makes it easier for the performance of whiteness – makes the same “old burnt cork blackface routine easier” (Obadike, 2001), and faster.

Although Obadike was one of the first net artists to perceive and comment on the colonial nature of the Web and its overpowering domination as a new resource to society, I believe that Obadike’s piece was not ahead of it’s time – rather a premonition of the way the digital space now commodifies and uses blackness. It showcases the early hesitancies that came with the freedom of a new, virtual and uncolonized space – and the way in which the move into an inseparable space of social media and physical living has recreated the relationship between blackness and profit – albeit in more than just money and resources – that is directly comparable to the black body in the historically colonial space.
