The Digital Divide

The prevalence of technology with the shift into the COVID pandemic has become paramount to the continuity of the working world, as well as in the means of youth education across the globe. However, the pre-existing, and ever expanding, digital divide presented by the vast difference in objects of affordances across various countries, has had a tumultuous effect on the already marginalized groups of various backgrounds, creating a need to address the lack of access in rural communities and disadvantaged minorities.

Research has shown that adequate internet access and considering the educational pairing needed for this, has more benefits to rural communities, and the countries of these rural communities (Daniolou, 2020). Not only would internet access create digital spaces for these cultures and peoples, but it would also serve well in cases of long-distance emergencies, general ease of communication and with the access to such vast information, the ability to breach gaps of social inclusion and increase community development. However, the impact of the capitalist notions on the pricing of internet access, plays a large role in hindering such developments and the fear of the potential, addressable dangers that come with such an affordance.

In the article by Daniolou, there is a brief discussion on the benefits and dangers of increasing access to areas that face the ‘rural penalty’ (Daniolou, 2020). Throughout, it is clear to see that most cons to the action of increasing access, can be dealt with adequate education and means to technological literacy. If the introduction of, specifically in this article, broadband is paired with the necessary information on how these systems function and what the capabilities of such technology are, there is a good chance that most of the concerns on misuse, misleading radicality and the dilution of existing cultural practices (Daniolou, 2020), can be addressed and resolved. Thus, the benefits, of which are plentiful, can truly begin to address the widening of the digital divide.

Tataki and Glynos’ report that with the unprecedented disruptions in the work and school spheres of society, there has been a drastic increase in the digital divide with the lack of access being disproportionately attributed to the most disadvantaged communities (Tataki & Glynos, 2020). Both students and workers affected by the shift to remote learning form part of societal minorities – with the majority affected worldwide being girls and women (Tataki & Glynos, 2020), and those within the US context mostly being from low-income families – who are also a part of the ethnic minorities of the country.

In the South African context, we saw this effect in the divide as the increase in job losses (Makgatho, 2021) and COVID deaths across the country forced many families into low-income states. While there was an increasing debate on who to blame, I believe it is not that hard to see how the lack of income and the increasing inflation has forced families, and minors to focus on household supporting, and in turn has directly impacted the highest number of dropouts in twenty years (Makgatho, 2021). Although we are aware of the government’s attempts to breach access barriers by relaying tablets and the like to state schools, we also see how little to no effort has been made in making sure that there is adequate broadband or internet access in these said areas.

Within our own South African context, we can see how the efforts are still being made, but also how the lack of holistic consideration has an effect. The gap presented by the digital divide is ever increasing with the state of the globe as we progress through the pandemic and tackle how to allow the permeation of information, education, and social interactions in the digital space, we also need to address how such advances and proposals further the gaps in access for rural populations and minorities in the working and educational spheres. Although there may be hesitancies with bridging these barriers, there exists multiple solutions that can minimize these fears, and create spaces of access for those who are receiving the short end of the pandemic stick.
