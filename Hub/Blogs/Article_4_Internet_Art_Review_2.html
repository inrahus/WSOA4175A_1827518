Analysis of ‘Blind Spot’

In Miao Ying’s piece, “Blind Spot”, Ying highlights concerns, and comments on the highly censored state of accessible internet in her home country China. What started as scholastic research, lead to the eventual creation of Ying’s physical, web affected art dictionary. Ying took on the intense task of individually searching each word in the dictionary and erasing along the way the words were censored and restricted by the country’s local laws. During the making of this piece, Ying aimed to place a spotlight on this rigid policing and the negative effects of the state’s laws that are perpetuated by communist ideals.

The existence of ‘Blind Spot’ can be considered an artifact as it crosses the two temporal spaces in its finality (Taylor, 2009). The dictionary itself, being a version pre-communist China, and its now censored state showcasing the new acceptable discourses and language. Ying’s elaborate address of this exploitation of the ideal of upholding the whole of society, also showcases the temporal shift from pre- to post-communist China (Net Art Anthology, 2007). However, this shift was not just that of the state, but also of the population’s acceptance of these censorship regulation, and what Ying refers to as ‘self-censorship’ (ELEPHANT, 2021) – something Ying relates to Stockholm Syndrome.

The annotated dictionary artwork crated by Ying was the gateway piece into her relationship with the power dynamics associated with censorship (Net Art Anthology, 2007) in her home country and her bridging into web art (ELEPHANT, 2021). Ying’s works in the net art sphere encase a certain methodology of address and comparison. Ying states how the works begin with a “digital core” (ELEPHANT, 2021) that finds relation to one of the country’s communist ideologies, and from there the abstraction begins. This is easily recognizable in her work, ‘Blind Spot’.

The digital core of the work, ‘Blind Spot’ would be the search engine, Google – or a search engine in general. This core is something used to access information of various discourses, spheres, and natures. Ying’s relation of this medium to the piece through the exploited ideal of ‘society’s best interest’ (Chu, Lau, Moriah, & Schallich, 2009), is abstracted through the exploration of censorship – the main way in which the interests of the whole are prioritized over the individual. Although this ideal is often associated with communism, and is the offered reason for the censorship, there is an extreme process behind the information that even is available for the public of China to access (Chu, Lau, Moriah, & Schallich, 2009).

As Ying moved away from China and to the US, there was a newfound frustration and a lot of Ying’s following net artworks after ‘Blind Spot’ began to address, with the previously mentioned methodology, various aspects of the way Chain’s political sphere was exploiting the ideals of communism to uphold their power. Although the initial scope of Ying’s work was to explore the limitations of the dynamics caused by censorship, there has also been a movement in recent works of Ying to address the more holistic aspect of the digital limitations on the human experience (ELEPHANT, 2021). This shift has not moved Ying from her focus on the censorship issues present in China, it has instead furthered her works and allowed for a more direct address of Ying’s scope on the effect of the digital control and it’s interrelation with the censorship issues in China.
